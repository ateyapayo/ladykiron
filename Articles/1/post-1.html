Invisible Women - Book review

28th December 2021

I read Perez’s book Invisible Women for a university project, but since it is an important piece of feminist literature, I thought it deserved a review here too! Overall I thought Perez’s book provided important insight on issues of equality today through a more scientific, ther than humanitarian or philosophical, lens. However, I had issues with the way in which the information throughout the book was presented to the reader.
If you are looking for a piece which can provide an encyclopaedia level of information, this is the book for you, but if you are looking for some deep and thorough reflections about that information, then you will have to do this yourself. And indeed this is perhaps what Perez intended the reader to do! However, for the common reader picking up this book in a book shop out of curiosity the amount of information and evidence presented about the lack of data collected and considered for women today presents itself as overwhelming. At times it seemed discouraging to continue reading the book when you knew you were going to be presented with more of the same, in this way the book lacked a dynamic narrative which kept you engaged. Albeit, the amount of evidence she presents definitely makes her argument (that women do not have enough data or information considered about them when structural and institutional changes are being made; that our world is thus not built for women) seem water-tight.

Exacerbating this flaw is that Perez does not take time to present many solutions to all the problems she presents to the reader, which again can seem discouraging, and frankly, depressing. She mentions in the last chapter that her next book will be about discussing actionable solutions that the reader can take to help solve these issues of equality, but I believe containing all this information in another book was a mistake. I doubt that many readers will feel drawn to pick up another book by Perez if they managed to make it to the end of this one, not because of problems with the content of the book itself (as I have said, the information she presents is valid and important in taking another view on issues of equality) but because of the way she presents this. It is for this reason that I found this book particularly frustrating, because had the information been packaged in a more readable way, I believe it could do great work in tackling feminism through science and data! Something which is new and different compared to any feminist literature I have read before. A few people who have read this book made an interesting comment that they believe this book is more palatable for male readers, due to the more hard, factual, and difficult to refute evidence that is presented about the gender gap. However, I believe stating this, that men are more scientifically minded compared to women and thus able to tolerate this type of literature much more, only insinuates further gendered stereotypes which ironically perpetuates the issues of gender inequality that the book is trying to alleviate.

Overall, give this book a go if you want a fresh perspective on modern-day feminism, but I’d suggest taking sufficient breaks in between each chapter to fully absorb all the information presented.
This book definitely isn’t a page turner!
